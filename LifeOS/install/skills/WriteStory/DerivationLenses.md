# Derivation Lenses

The Layering Interview derives a story's spine from whatever the writer brings. There is no single correct order to derive in. Sacred-flaw-first (Storr) is one lens, and it's the right one for character-arc fiction — but it distorts plot-driven genre, literary, ensemble, and mood-led work. Pick the lens that fits the seed, the genre, and where the writer started.

The seven layers are always the destination. The lens only sets the **order** you reach them in.

## The Five Lenses

| Lens | Use when the seed/genre is… | Derives in this order |
|------|------------------------------|------------------------|
| **Psychology-first** | Character-arc fiction, literary character study, most novels | False belief → origin → dramatic question → want/need → opposition → theme → plot as the test |
| **Structure-first** | Genre, thriller, mystery, procedural, action — plot-driven | Hook-Build-Payoff structure / moral argument → characters fitted to the roles → flaw discovered inside the structure |
| **Premise-first** | Idea fiction, argument-driven stories | A cause→effect premise sentence → characters built to prove it → events that test it |
| **Theme-emergent** | Literary, experimental, slice-of-life | Hold theme loose; build scenes and characters; let the theme surface, then name it late |
| **Resonance-first** | Vibe / image / mood seeds with no agent yet | Texture, image, what's-not-there — elicit material before deriving any causal spine |

State which lens you're using and why in one line. You can switch lenses if the writer's material pulls a different way — the writer's instinct wins.

## Psychology-First (Storr, Cron)

See `StorrFramework.md` for the full sacred-flaw engine. The short version: false belief → origin damage → dramatic question (derived from the flaw) → want vs. need → opposition/pressure → theme. Plot comes last, built as the machine that tests the belief.

### Lisa Cron's *Story Genius* — the sibling method

Cron's method is psychology-first too, more granular at the scene level:

- **Misbelief** — a flawed worldview, usually formed in childhood, that limits the protagonist. (Storr's sacred flaw by another name.) Example: "showing weakness leads to rejection."
- **The Third Rail** — the live wire connecting the protagonist's **desire** and their **misbelief**. It powers every scene. A scene with no third-rail charge is dead.
- **The "why"** — Cron's relentless demand: not just what the character wants and what blocks it, but *why*, traced to a specific origin moment.
- **The misbelief origin scene** — write the formative moment the misbelief was born, early.

Cron's rule for the whole book: **every external obstacle should create or intensify an internal dilemma tied to the misbelief.** She also makes the writer's own stake a step — "why do you care about this story?" — which is worth asking.

## Structure-First (Pressfield, Truby)

When the writer leads with plot or genre, derive the structure first and fit character into it. Use Pressfield/Coyne's **Hook-Build-Payoff** (see `PressfieldFramework.md`) as the scaffold, and Truby's character-arc engine inside it.

### John Truby's *The Anatomy of Story*

- **Every story is a moral argument.** The story argues its theme by dramatizing characters' choices and their consequences — not by stating a message. The hero pursues a goal by questionable means, meets an opponent who embodies a competing value, and through self-revelation arrives at the right way to act. That journey *is* the argument.
- **The designing principle** — the single line that organizes the whole story and makes it original (e.g., "a man becomes a woman to learn to be a better man").
- **The 7 key steps** (the character-arc spine to layer inside the acts):
  1. **Weakness and Need** — something missing in the hero, profound enough to be ruining their life (often a moral need, not just psychological).
  2. **Desire** — the concrete external goal the audience follows.
  3. **Opponent** — the character who most wants to stop the hero, ideally attacking the hero's greatest weakness.
  4. **Plan** — the strategy the hero uses to reach the goal.
  5. **Battle** — the final, escalated conflict.
  6. **Self-Revelation** — the hero sees themselves honestly for the first time (psychological + moral).
  7. **New Equilibrium** — things settle at a permanently higher or lower level.

## Premise-First (Egri)

For idea-driven and argument stories, start from the **premise**: a compressed cause→effect sentence that is the seed the whole story grows from. Lajos Egri's examples:

- "Great love defies even death." (Romeo and Juliet)
- "Frugality leads to waste."
- "Jealousy destroys itself and the object of its love." (Othello)

A well-formed premise implies three things: a **character** trait, a **conflict**, and a **conclusion**. "Frugality [character] leads to [conflict] waste [conclusion]." The verb is the engine. For Egri, character is primary — well-built, three-dimensional characters drive the plot themselves, and every scene must prove the premise or get cut.

**Use it in the interview:** if the writer leads with an idea or argument, help them compress it into one cause→effect sentence, then build characters whose collision proves it.

## Theme-Emergent and Resonance-First

Some stories should NOT have their theme or spine derived up front:

- **Theme-emergent** (literary, experimental): hold the theme loose. Build scenes and characters that interest the writer, watch what keeps recurring, and name the theme late — applied over a story that already works, not bolted under it.
- **Resonance-first** (vibe/image/mood seeds): there's no agent or causality yet, so deriving a flaw and a plot is fabrication. Stay in the mood. Explore texture, images, what's deliberately absent, what the feeling reminds the writer of. Use the **Explore** workflow to pull divergent material out of the writer until there's enough of their own substrate to build a spine from — then switch to the lens that fits what emerged.

## The Rule Underneath All of Them

Whatever the lens, the move is the same: **derive from the writer's material, present every element as a labeled proposal, and let them ratify, redirect, or reject.** The lens decides the order; the writer decides the content.
