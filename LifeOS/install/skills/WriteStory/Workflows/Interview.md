# Interview Workflow — The Layering Interview

Take what the writer already has and build the rest of the story with them, one layer at a time. This is the entry point for any writer who has a seed — a single line, a folder of notes, a half-drafted chapter — and wants help turning it into something whole.

## The Stance (read first)

This workflow augments a creator. It never substitutes for one.

- Read the writer's material **before** asking anything. Every question references something they actually gave you.
- Never invent the spine. Derive it **from their seed**, and present every piece as a **proposal they ratify**, not a fact you assert.
- Label the source of every proposal: **"inferred from your seed: <their words>"** vs. **"a new option I'm offering."** The writer must always be able to tell which is which.
- Offer **options to choose among**, not a single answer to nod at. Approving a plausible suggestion is not the same as creating it.
- The writer's word wins. A redirect is the truth, not a deviation.

## Step 1: Seed Intake

The writer can bring anything. Accept all of it.

- **A single line or paragraph** — read it.
- **A directory of material** — the writer points the interview at a folder (notes, sketches, outlines, draft scenes, world-building). **Read ALL of it first**, before any questions. Then explore with them how to proceed from what's actually there.
- **An existing project** — if `LIFEOS/USER/CUSTOMIZATIONS/SKILLS/WriteStory/projects/<book-slug>/` exists, read the saved `interview.md` and `ISA.md` and resume from the current state instead of starting over.

Then classify the **seed class** — it decides how you proceed:

| Seed class | What they brought |
|------------|-------------------|
| **Character** | A person, a flaw, a voice |
| **Premise** | A "what if", a situation, a hook |
| **Plot** | A sequence of events, a structure |
| **Theme** | An argument, an idea, a question they want to explore |
| **World** | A setting, a system, a place |
| **Vibe / image** | A mood, a feeling, an image, a fragment — no agent or causality yet |

## Step 2: Reflect Back, Find the Heat

Before deriving anything, show the writer you heard them.

- **Play it back in their own words.** "Here's the story I think you're telling — did I get it right?" Let them correct you. This is where trust is built.
- **Find the heat.** Ask: *"What excites you most about this?"* and *"What do you want a reader to feel?"* Wherever their energy is — that's the layer to deepen first.

If the seed is **vibe/image only**, do NOT proceed to derivation. Stay in resonance mode: explore texture, images, what's NOT in the picture, what the mood reminds them of. Pull material **out** of the writer (use the Explore workflow for divergent options) until there's enough of their own substrate to build on. Fabricating a flaw and a plot from a mood, then asking them to "confirm" it, is substitution wearing a mask.

## Step 3: Pick a Derivation Lens

There is no single correct spine. Sacred-flaw-first works for character-arc fiction and fails for plenty of other stories. Pick the lens that fits the seed class, the genre, and where the writer started. Full detail in `DerivationLenses.md`.

| Lens | Best for | Spine it derives |
|------|----------|------------------|
| **Psychology-first** (Storr / Cron) | Character-arc fiction, literary character studies | Sacred flaw / misbelief → dramatic question → plot as its test |
| **Structure-first** (Pressfield / Truby) | Genre, thriller, procedural, plot-driven | Hook-Build-Payoff / moral argument → character fitted to it |
| **Premise-first** (Egri) | Argument-driven, idea fiction | Cause→effect premise sentence → characters that prove it |
| **Theme-emergent** | Literary, experimental, slice-of-life | Hold theme loose; let it surface from scenes, derive it late |
| **Resonance-first** | Vibe / image / mood seeds | Texture and image before any causal spine |

State which lens you're using and why, in one line. The seven layers are still the destination — the lens only sets the **order** you reach them in.

## Step 4: Derive the Spine as Proposals

Using the chosen lens, derive each spine element **with the writer**, one at a time, each as a labeled proposal they confirm, redirect, or reject.

For the psychology-first lens, the chain is:

1. **The false belief** (Storr's sacred flaw / Cron's misbelief) — the thing the character clings to that isn't true. Hold it to precision: not "she's insecure" but "she believes she's the only adult in any room."
2. **Origin damage** — the specific past moment that planted the belief.
3. **The dramatic question** — derived from the flaw automatically: *"will they stay [flaw] or become [not-flaw]?"* This is the engine of the whole book.
4. **Want vs. need** — the want is what they chase; the need is usually the inverse of the flaw.
5. **The opposition / pressure** — what forces the question. **Not just a "villain."** It can be a person, a system, fate, society, time, an institution, or the character's own contradiction. Pick the form that actually presses on this flaw.
6. **Theme, in one sentence** — the controlling idea (Pressfield) / premise (Egri) / moral argument (Truby). For theme-emergent lenses, leave this open and revisit it later.

For other lenses, derive the equivalent spine in that lens's order (see `DerivationLenses.md`).

**Every derivation follows the same move:** *"You gave me X. That implies Y. Does that land?"* — with the provenance labeled. When you're unsure, offer two or three contrasting candidates and let the writer pick. This is the layering: the writer sees the hidden structure already implied by their own idea, and chooses.

## Step 5: Layer Outward — One Dimension Per Pass

Once the spine (meaning + character, or the chosen lens's core) is confirmed, deepen the remaining layers in dependency order — each as a focused pass over what already exists:

1. **Plot** — the engine built to test the flaw (every major beat should press on it)
2. **Mystery** — what the reader knows vs. doesn't, and the gaps that pull them forward
3. **World** — the setting and rules, shaped to put pressure on the flaw
4. **Relationships** — especially the influence character who most challenges the flaw
5. **Prose** — voice and aesthetic (anchor to the favorites in Step 6)

For each layer:
- **Where the writer has material**, elicit and deepen it — Maass-style, point at their own pages: *"Where's the tension in the next few seconds of this scene?"* / *"What is your point-of-view character anxious about in this paragraph?"* The writer answers; the depth is theirs.
- **Where the writer is genuinely blank**, offer options via the **Explore** workflow. Never fill a layer silently.

## Step 6: Anchor Aesthetic and Reader Target

Two short prompts that shape the Prose layer and set the verification target:

- *"What are 3-5 stories (books, films, shows, games) you love, and what specifically did you love about each?"* — extracts aesthetic, thematic, and structural taste, and gives a voice to anchor to.
- *"When someone finishes your story, what do you want them to feel?"* — this becomes a top-line criterion in the Story Bible.

## Step 7: Cheap, Disposable First Pass

Don't demand a complete bible before anything exists. Produce a **one-page rough spine fast** — theme, dramatic question, hook, climax, the three acts (Pressfield's Hook-Build-Payoff). Mark it explicitly disposable. The point is momentum: the writer sees a whole beginning-middle-end early, which beats the Resistance that kills projects.

Reduce decision fatigue. Offer sane defaults and an escape hatch ("want me to just propose the next three beats?") rather than forcing the writer to make a meta-decision at every turn. Blocked writers need fewer choices, not more.

## Step 8: Save Project State

Write the results to the project directory:

```
LIFEOS/USER/CUSTOMIZATIONS/SKILLS/WriteStory/projects/<book-slug>/interview.md
```

Capture: seed class, the lens used, the confirmed spine (with provenance), per-layer state (confirmed / partial / open), the favorites + reader target, and the one-page Foolscap spine. This file is the resume point — next session, the interview re-reads it and continues.

The book's living plan lives next to it as `ISA.md` (created/grown by **BuildBible**). Interview produces the spine; BuildBible turns it into the project ISA.

## Step 9: Handoff

Reflect the assembled spine back, name what's **confirmed** vs. still **open**, and let the writer choose the next step:

- **Deepen a layer** that's still thin → **Explore**
- **Build the full structured plan** → **BuildBible** (writes the project ISA)
- **Start writing the strongest scene now** → **WriteChapter**

The writer drives. Your job was to fill in the pieces they were missing — not to decide the story.
